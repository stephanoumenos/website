My most productive days are now indistinguishable from my laziest. I type for perhaps 45 minutes across an eight-hour stretch. I ship more than I used to in a week.

## The geometry

Traditional work traces a sawtooth: output scales linearly with keyboard time, interrupted by breaks. Agentic work traces a step function: idle stretches punctuated by bursts where parallel agents spawn subagents and output grows superlinearly.

Two constraints keep the model honest. Amdahl's law: a serial fraction _s_ caps speedup at 1/(_s_ + (1 − _s_)/_p_), so adding agents hits diminishing returns fast. And a failure probability per cycle, because agents hallucinate. Increase it and the confidence band widens until the agentic curve crosses below traditional. That crossover arrives sooner than most people expect.

## The critic who stopped painting

The skill this regime demands most — code judgement — is exactly the one that atrophies fastest when you stop writing code yourself. The dangerous failures are never syntax errors. They are structural: a wrong abstraction, a race condition that only manifests under load, an API surface that will be painful to extend in six months. Code that _works_ but that an experienced engineer _feels_ is off.

That feeling comes from reps. From having written bad abstractions and lived with the consequences. You are now reading an order of magnitude more code than you write, which means your judgement must outpace your practice. The workflow that maximises your output today is quietly eroding the skill that makes you productive at all.

## Vigilance, not endurance

Two minutes on, twenty minutes off. Every instinct says procrastination. The only evidence otherwise is the commit log.

The mode is less marathon runner, more air traffic controller. You must go from zero to full engagement in seconds when an agent finishes, evaluate a complex diff, make a judgement call, and let go again. The difficulty is not the interventions themselves but staying sharp enough to make them well when your fourteenth agent of the day hands you diff number thirty.

The people who will thrive here are not the fastest typists or the most focused. They are the ones who can tolerate feeling unproductive while being extremely productive — a weirdly specific psychological trait that no one has started selecting for, and no existing productivity framework accounts for.
