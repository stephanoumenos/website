I became vegan for the animals. I stayed vegan because it became who I am.

The argument is simple enough: if it's wrong to cause unnecessary suffering, and we can live well
without eating animals, then we should. Once you take that seriously, it's hard to argue with.

But logic alone doesn't sustain a lifestyle. What sustains it is finding people who live it,
cooking food you genuinely love, and letting it become ordinary.

## The first month

The first month was hard — not because of cravings, exactly, but because of social friction.
Every meal with friends became a negotiation. Every restaurant menu became a puzzle.
I felt conspicuous in a way I hadn't anticipated.

What helped was reframing the question. Instead of asking *what can I not eat*, I started asking
*what do I actually want to eat*. That shift made all the difference.

## What nobody tells you

Nobody tells you that going vegan will make you a better cook. When you remove the default
options — the quick chicken, the easy burger — you're forced to actually think about food.
You start exploring cuisines that were always mostly plant-based: Indian, Ethiopian, Thai, Lebanese.

You start paying attention in a way you never did before.

## On purity

One thing I've learned: veganism doesn't have to be about purity. It's about reducing harm where
you reasonably can. The perfect shouldn't be the enemy of the good.

If someone eats plant-based 80% of the time, that matters. If they slip up, that's fine. The goal
isn't a clean record — it's a lighter footprint.

I say this as someone who wasted years being self-righteous about it. Don't be that person.
